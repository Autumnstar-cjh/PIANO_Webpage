Abstract

Solar magnetic field extrapolation is essential for understanding solar activity and space weather events such as flares and coronal mass ejections. A widely used approach is the Nonlinear Force-Free Field (NLFFF) approximation, which reconstructs the three-dimensional coronal magnetic field from observed surface magnetograms. While effective, traditional NLFFF solvers rely on iterative numerical methods that are computationally expensive and difficult to apply in real-time settings.

PIANO (Physics-Informed Attention-enhanced Fourier Neural Operator) is designed to accelerate NLFFF extrapolation without sacrificing physical consistency. Instead of solving nonlinear equations iteratively, PIANO learns a direct mapping from boundary magnetic field observations to the full 3D coronal field using Fourier neural operator layers. The model supports multimodal input, combining image-based boundary data with physical scalar parameters, and integrates attention mechanisms to effectively fuse this information.

To ensure physically meaningful predictions, PIANO incorporates divergence-free and force-free constraints directly into training. A two-phase training strategy further improves volumetric consistency. Experimental results demonstrate that PIANO achieves state-of-the-art accuracy while significantly reducing computational cost, making fast and reliable NLFFF extrapolation feasible for large-scale studies and potential real-time applications.

Model Overview

PIANO (Physics-Informed Attention-enhanced Fourier Neural Operator) is designed to efficiently extrapolate the 3D coronal magnetic field from observed 2D boundary magnetograms, while enforcing key physical laws.

It maps boundary magnetic field data Bx(x,y), By(x,y), Bz(x,y) to a volumetric 3D magnetic field Bx(x,y,z), By(x,y,z), Bz(x,y,z).

Multimodal Input Lifting. The observed magnetic field is first lifted into a high-dimensional feature space using a small neural network (MLP). In parallel, scalar physical parameters (domain lengths in x, y, and z) are processed through a separate lifting network. These features are refined using a lightweight attention mechanism and then combined to form the model’s initial representation.

Fourier Neural Operator Layers. The combined features are passed through a sequence of Fourier layers. These layers operate in frequency space, allowing the model to learn global spatial interactions efficiently. This enables PIANO to model long-range magnetic field dependencies with significantly lower computational cost than traditional solvers.

Projection to 3D Magnetic Field. A final projection layer maps the learned features back to the physical magnetic field components Bx, By, and Bz throughout the 3D volume.

Physics-Informed Training. During training, the model is constrained to produce physically consistent magnetic fields. Two physics-based penalties are enforced: (1) a divergence-free condition, and (2) a force-free condition. These constraints ensure that predictions remain physically plausible.

Two-Phase Training Strategy. PIANO is trained in two stages. First, the model learns from 2D boundary data with physics constraints. In the second phase, the model is retrained using its own 3D predictions as input, further improving volumetric consistency.

Results

Comparison of predicted magnetic field components and corresponding error maps across multiple neural operator models at different heights (2–16 Mm).

Errors are largest near the lower boundary (2–4 Mm) and decrease with height. While most state-of-the-art models perform well on Bx and Bz, the By component presents greater structural complexity and is more challenging to extrapolate. PIANO demonstrates competitive accuracy at lower heights and superior performance at higher heights, particularly for By.

Bx Component

Most neural operator models achieve accurate extrapolation for Bx, with errors concentrated near lower heights (2–4 Mm).

Prediction accuracy improves as height increases, reflecting smoother magnetic structures away from the boundary.

By Component

The By structure is more complex than Bx and Bz, resulting in larger and more persistent extrapolation errors across heights.

PIANO maintains stronger accuracy at higher heights, demonstrating clearer structural consistency compared to other models.

Bz Component

Similar to Bx, most models achieve strong reconstruction performance for Bz, with higher errors concentrated near the lower boundary.

Error magnitude decreases with height, indicating improved agreement with ground truth in upper layers.
